# Email Deliverability & Rendering Review — tripcast

## Overall verdict

**Adequate, trending thin on execution-critical details.** The spine gets the big structural calls right — 600px single-column, table layout with `role="presentation"`, inline styles, web-safe fonts, no shadows, no CSS grid, square-corner fallback, mandatory plain-text part, monochrome glyphs over emoji, and a calm/never-alarmist posture that is genuinely deliverability-friendly. But for a product where *the email is the product*, the spine is silent or hand-wavy on the things that decide whether the email lands in the inbox and renders correctly: the icon **delivery method** is left undefined (the single highest rendering risk), there is **no dark-mode strategy beyond declaring tokens** (no inversion handling, no `meta`/`color-scheme`), and core deliverability mechanics (**preheader, subject-line strategy, sender identity, `List-Unsubscribe` / `List-Unsubscribe-Post` headers, SPF/DKIM/DMARC, one-click HTTP unsubscribe**) are entirely absent. These are not implementation trivia for this product — they are the spine's actual subject matter, and right now they are gaps.

## Findings

**[critical]** Weather-glyph delivery method is unspecified (DESIGN.md §Components "Forecast day-row"; EXPERIENCE.md §Accessibility Floor). The spine mandates "monochrome line-style weather glyphs" with `alt` text but never says *how they ship*. Inline SVG is effectively unusable in email — Gmail (all surfaces), Outlook (all), and Yahoo strip or refuse to render `<svg>`. Icon fonts fail under image-blocking and dark mode. This is the single biggest rendering risk and the spine is silent on it. *Fix:* Mandate **hosted PNG (2x retina, served at 1x dimensions) with meaningful `alt` and explicit `width`/`height`**, on an always-on CDN; forbid inline SVG and icon fonts. Specify a non-image fallback: because images are blocked by default in Outlook desktop and often elsewhere, every glyph's meaning MUST also live in the adjacent text ("Rain likely") so the row is fully legible with images off — which the spine already requires for a11y; make it an explicit image-blocking-resilience rule.

**[critical]** No deliverability/authentication spec at all (both files). For a bulk-sending product subject to the Gmail/Yahoo Feb-2024 bulk-sender rules and CAN-SPAM, the spine never mentions **SPF, DKIM, DMARC (p=quarantine/reject with aligned domain), one-click `List-Unsubscribe` + `List-Unsubscribe-Post: List-Unsubscribe=One-Click` headers, a dedicated sending domain/subdomain, reverse DNS, or a <0.3% spam-complaint ceiling.** The product cannot function without these and they belong in the spine, not "implied." *Fix:* Add a "Deliverability invariants" section: authenticated dedicated subdomain with SPF+DKIM+DMARC alignment; `List-Unsubscribe` (mailto + HTTPS) and `List-Unsubscribe-Post` headers on every send so Gmail/Apple render native unsubscribe; honor unsubscribe within the required window; monitor Google Postmaster reputation; keep complaint rate under 0.3%.

**[high]** Dark mode is declared but not handled (DESIGN.md §Colors dark tokens; EXPERIENCE.md §Responsive "Dark-mode-aware … never assume a white background"). Declaring dark tokens is not a dark-mode strategy. The spine doesn't address the three distinct client behaviors: (1) **Apple Mail / iOS** respects `@media (prefers-color-scheme)` and `<meta name="color-scheme">` / `color-scheme: light dark` — you can target it; (2) **Outlook.com / Outlook mobile** *forcibly invert* colors and ignore your media queries, partially remapping near-white/near-black — your declared dark surfaces will be re-inverted unpredictably; (3) **Gmail Android** applies forced-dark to emails with light backgrounds, mangling them. The same hosted PNG glyphs will also become invisible on dark backgrounds (dark line-art on dark surface). *Fix:* Specify `<meta name="color-scheme" content="light dark">` + `color-scheme:light dark;supported-color-schemes:light dark;`, a `prefers-color-scheme` media block for Apple/iOS, design glyphs that read on both surfaces (PNG with a mid-tone stroke or paired light/dark image swap via the media query) or add a subtle padded "halo"/`surface-raised` chip behind each glyph so forced-inversion can't erase it. Explicitly accept that Outlook.com inversion is partial and test the worst case.

**[high]** No subject-line or preheader (preview text) strategy (both files). The preheader is the single most-read piece of an email after the subject and is invisible in the layout if unspecified — clients will scrape the first visible text (likely "View in browser" or the place name) into the preview. For an email-is-the-product daily send, subject + preheader *are* the open-rate engine, and the never-alarmist constraint applies most sharply here. *Fix:* Specify a subject-line pattern that mirrors the calm header ("5 days until Edinburgh" / "Day 2 in Edinburgh" — never "⚠️ Rain alert"), a hidden preheader block (~40–100 chars, hidden-but-present with a trailing whitespace/zero-width spacer so trailing body text doesn't leak into the preview), and ban alarmist/all-caps/emoji-spam subjects (which also hurt spam scoring).

**[high]** Sender identity is unspecified (both files). No `From` display name, sender address, or `Reply-To` is defined. Sender name is a primary trust/deliverability signal and recipients filter on it. *Fix:* Define a consistent friendly `From` name (e.g. "tripcast") on the authenticated sending domain, a monitored or explicitly no-reply `Reply-To`, and a stable physical-postal-address line in the footer (CAN-SPAM requirement — currently missing from the footer spec, which only lists feedback + unsubscribe).

**[medium]** Feedback chips use emoji (👍 / 👎) as functional UI in the digest footer (DESIGN.md §Components; EXPERIENCE.md §Component Patterns). The spine bans multicolor weather emoji for cross-client unpredictability but then relies on 👍/👎 emoji for an actual interaction. These render inconsistently (monochrome vs. color, platform-variant glyphs, missing on some Outlook/Windows configurations) and under image-blocking they're fine (they're text) but visually erratic — and the chips are *links*, so they must be tap-legible. *Fix:* Pair each chip with a text label ("👍 This helped" / "👎 Not helpful" — EXPERIENCE.md already implies the labels; make the visible text mandatory so the control works even if the emoji glyph is absent/odd), and verify the chips are ≥44px tap targets in the email, not just on web.

**[medium]** `rounded/full` pill CTAs and feedback chips in email (DESIGN.md §Shapes, §Components). The spine correctly notes large radii are inconsistent and says treat rounding as progressive enhancement — but then specifies `rounded/full` pills for the email feedback chips and allows pill CTAs. Outlook (Word engine) ignores `border-radius` entirely (square), and a `full`-radius pill that falls back to a sharp rectangle looks broken, not just "less rounded." *Fix:* Cap email button/chip radius at `sm`–`md` (already the stated container rule — extend it to interactive elements), or use the VML roundrect technique for Outlook bulletproof buttons. Reserve `rounded/full` for web only.

**[medium]** Plain-text fallback is mandated but its *contents* aren't specified (DESIGN.md Do/Don't; EXPERIENCE.md Foundation + Accessibility Floor "plain-text fallback is itself a full accessible alternative"). "Paired with every send" and "a full accessible alternative" is the right intent, but nothing enumerates what must appear: countdown/position line, all 7 days with conditions + high/low in °F **and** °C + precip %, the feedback action (as tappable URLs, since text can't render chips), the unsubscribe/end-trip URL, and the physical address. A weak text part also *raises spam score* (Gmail penalizes HTML-only and HTML/text-mismatch sends). *Fix:* Specify the plain-text part as a structured, content-complete mirror with literal URLs for feedback and unsubscribe, the same numbers as the HTML, and the "limited data today" line behavior — not a stripped afterthought.

**[medium]** No `prefers-reduced-motion` / animation concern is moot in email, but **no image-blocking resilience rule for the header/identity** (DESIGN.md §Components "Digest email"). The place name is specified as `display` *type* (good — it's live text, not an image), but if any header image/logo is later added it must degrade. More importantly, with Outlook desktop blocking images by default, the entire forecast (glyph-dependent) must remain readable. *Fix:* State explicitly: no information may live in an image alone; all glyphs carry `alt` + adjacent text; no background-image used for layout or content (Outlook ignores `background-image` without VML); the email must be fully comprehensible with all images suppressed.

**[medium]** Signed/scoped links are specified behaviorally but not for email-client safety (EXPERIENCE.md §Component Patterns, §Interaction Primitives "signed, scoped links"). Good that unsubscribe/feedback/magic-link are signed and login-free. But two email-specific risks aren't addressed: (1) **link scanners / security appliances** (Outlook Safe Links, Proofpoint, Mimecast) and Gmail's prefetch will *pre-click* GET links, which can silently consume a one-time magic link or fire feedback/unsubscribe without user intent; (2) the unsubscribe must be reachable as a true one-click. *Fix:* Magic-link, feedback, and end-trip actions should not perform a destructive/state-changing mutation on a bare GET from a scanner — use a confirmation landing (already implied for unsubscribe via the landing page) or POST-on-confirm, and ensure the `List-Unsubscribe-Post` one-click path is idempotent and scanner-safe.

**[low]** Font-size minimums for mobile are defined but not flagged as iOS-autosize-safe (DESIGN.md §Typography). Body 16px / meta 13px / temp 18px are reasonable, but **iOS Mail/Gmail auto-upscale text below ~13–16px**, and the `meta` 13px footnote/unsubscribe line sits at the boundary. Tiny unsubscribe text can also read as a dark-pattern to filters. *Fix:* Keep body ≥16px (good), keep the unsubscribe/footer line legible (≥13–14px) and not greyed below contrast AA on the actual rendered (possibly inverted) background; confirm no email text falls under 13px.

**[low]** Retina / max-width on high-DPI not addressed for the 600px container (DESIGN.md §Layout "max content width 600px"). 600px is the correct cap, but on retina the hosted PNG glyphs need 2x source served at 1x box to stay crisp; the container should use `width:100%;max-width:600px` fluid (not a fixed 600px table) so it doesn't overflow narrow viewports. *Fix:* Specify fluid `max-width:600px` with `width:100%`, and 2x glyph assets constrained by explicit `width`/`height`.

**[low]** Never-alarmist constraint is strong in-body but not extended to the inbox-list surface (DESIGN.md §Brand; EXPERIENCE.md §Voice). The calm posture (rain = soft blue, no red, no warning badges, no alert styling) is excellent and well-defended for the email *body*. But the parts a user sees *before opening* — subject, preheader, sender name — are where alarm most easily creeps in and are currently unspecified (see high findings above). The constraint is sound; it just needs to reach the envelope. *Fix:* Apply the never-alarmist rule explicitly to subject + preheader (no "⚠", no "ALERT", no all-caps, no urgency punctuation), which also improves spam scoring.
